## Motivation
The goal is agents that operate a computer the way a human does — <Problem>keyboard and mouse with natural-language goals, in principle generalizable to any digital task</Problem>. The gap: at MiniWob's conception (<Related>Shi et al. 2017</Related>), the BC+RL combination over mouse/keyboard <Problem>did not produce competent agents</Problem> — with only ~17 hours of human data, pure behavioural cloning solved just ~5% more tasks than a random policy (p.4). Prior work therefore bolted on <Problem>DOM-specific actions, curricula, and constrained/workflow-guided exploration</Problem> (Liu 2018; Gur 2018; Jia 2019) to get results, leaving open whether that machinery was actually necessary.

## Contribution (what's new)
The genuine delta is empirical, not algorithmic: <Novelty>the decisive missing factor was the *scale* of human keyboard/mouse demonstrations, not specialized DOM actions or curricula</Novelty>. The design must keep the interface human-aligned (<Term def="mouse + keyboard actions, RGB pixels + DOM tree observations, NL goals">mouse/keyboard over pixels+DOM</Term>) precisely *because* that makes raw human demonstrations directly usable as training data — which is the resource being scaled. The authors explicitly frame this as resolving the original MiniWob hypothesis that conventional deep RL plus demonstrations would suffice (p.8-9).

## Research questions
Is the specialized machinery (DOM actions, curricula, guided exploration) of prior web agents actually necessary, or was the earlier negative result simply caused by too little human demonstration data? And does a single human-aligned agent co-trained on all tasks transfer across them?

## Methodology
A single multimodal agent (CC-Net) perceives 165x220 RGB pixels plus a processed DOM tree and the NL instruction, and emits human-equivalent mouse/keyboard actions. It is co-trained from scratch on all 104 tasks with an equally-weighted mixture of <Term def="behavioural cloning: supervised imitation of human action sequences">BC</Term> and RL (<Term def="V-MPO: an on-policy maximum-a-posteriori policy-optimization RL algorithm, Song et al. 2019">VMPO</Term>), using a demonstration set scaled ~400x beyond the original MiniWob data.

Action space: 10 action types — no-op; 7 mouse (move, click, double-click, press, release, wheel up/down); 2 keyboard (key press incl. macros like `CTRL+C`, emit a task-field string). Deliberately *no* DOM-element actions in the baseline; those exist only as an ablation (p.3-4, Table 2 p.12).

Data + training: 2.4M+ demonstrations across 104 tasks from 77 participants (~6300 h), crowdsourced and ethically reviewed; split 2.2M train / 310K test; episodes with final reward `< 0.5` filtered (~5%); runs of consecutive no-ops cleaved up to 10 (p.4-5). Equal-weight BC+RL from scratch; for smaller-data scaling runs the BC weight is scaled by `sqrt(relative dataset size)` to slow overfitting (p.6-7). Environment: custom C++ stack running Chrome over X11 with the Chrome DevTools Protocol, jailed in Sandbox2 with network rerouted to a local proxy — motivated by safe crowdsourced data collection (p.3).

## Main result
<Finding src="Fig.3, p.2,5">CC-Net reaches state-of-the-art and human-level *mean* performance across the 104 MiniWob++ tasks, exceeding aggregated external SotA "by a wide margin" — and does so without environment augmentations.</Finding> "Human-level" is a mean across tasks, not per-task parity: humans still beat the agent on several tasks while the agent beats humans on others.

## Key findings
- <Finding src="Fig.8, p.7-8">Removing the DOM observation drops mean performance ~75%</Finding>: the agent is strongly DOM-reliant, whereas removing pixels hurts far less — because the MiniWob "canvas" is cleanly encoded in the DOM, a benchmark artifact that would not hold for general computer control.
- <Finding src="Fig.7, p.6-7">Performance scales continuously across ~3 orders of magnitude of demonstration data</Finding>: ~400x the original ~17 h is needed to reach human level; using 1/1000 of the set (~6 h) overfits rapidly and gives no significant gain over RL-only.
- <Finding src="Fig.5, p.5-6">Co-training all 104 tasks is significantly more data-efficient per task than single-task training</Finding>: evidence of genuine cross-task transfer rather than pure memorization.
- <Finding src="Tab.3, p.14-15">The agent effectively fails exactly two tasks — simon-says (-0.00 vs 0.62 human) and terminal (-0.01 vs 0.88)</Finding>: simon-says fails because the agent observes at ~2Hz and misses parts of the sequence.
- <Finding src="Tab.3, p.14-15">The agent's biggest "win" over humans, moving-items (0.88 vs 0.18), comes from human control latency</Finding>, not superior competence — illustrating that the mean parity is partly inflated by tasks where the crowdworkers do poorly.
- <Finding src="Fig.8, p.8">Removing the privileged task-field action still solves form-filling</Finding>: the agent learns to highlight and drag text instead; DOM-element actions underperform the mouse/keyboard baseline and add no boost.

## Implications
This is a load-bearing data point for the modern computer-use-agent line: it argues that a generic perception-to-mouse/keyboard policy plus scaled human data is the recipe, reframing later debates about demonstrations vs. specialized affordances. It overturns the negative result of <Related>World of Bits / original MiniWob (Shi 2017)</Related>, contrasts with DOM-action and workflow-guided lines (<Related>Liu 2018, Gur 2018, Jia 2019</Related>), and sits methodologically downstream of <Related>large-scale-demonstration + RL work (DeepMind Interactive Agents 2021, AlphaStar)</Related>. <FollowUp>Whether the recipe survives a true pixel-only setting without a DOM-encoded canvas</FollowUp> and <FollowUp>how WebGPT and later WebArena/Mind2Web-style benchmarks moved the field past scripted, low-noise rewards</FollowUp> are the threads to chase.

## Limitations
- <Caveat>"Human-level" is a mean over 104 tasks against a specific crowdworker population; per-task humans still win on several, and some agent wins stem from human latency disadvantages.</Caveat>
- <Caveat>Heavy DOM dependence (~75% drop without it) plus the canvas-in-DOM artifact means true pixel-only / non-DOM computer control is not demonstrated.</Caveat>
- <Caveat>Only one seed per reported condition, hand-tuned, with no variance/statistical reporting despite high per-task variance.</Caveat>
- <Caveat>Large proprietary compute, infra, and demonstration data; no code/data/model release; total parameter count and compute cost Not reported.</Caveat>
- <Caveat>MiniWob++ sidesteps the hard intent/language/preference problem via scripted goals and programmatic low-noise rewards (authors note, p.8).</Caveat>

## Critiques and Questions
1. The external-SotA aggregate sets non-reported tasks to zero and filters 16 of 104 tasks, which can flatter the "wide margin" — how much of the headline gap is real vs. an artifact of the aggregation?
2. With a single seed and hand-tuning, how stable is "human-level mean" run-to-run, given the authors' own note of high per-task variance?
3. Given the 75% DOM-reliance, is the scaling story really about *general computer control*, or specifically about a benchmark whose visual content is readable from the DOM?

### Personal notes
Watch the framing trap: "human-level" = mean across 104 tasks, and some agent wins are due to human latency disadvantage (moving-items 0.88 vs 0.18). The DOM-reliance ablation (~75% drop) is the most important caveat for anyone wanting *pixel-only* computer use — MiniWob++'s canvas being in the DOM makes the benchmark easier than general GUIs.
