## Motivation
Off-the-shelf VLMs are <Problem>under-trained on decision-centric data</Problem>, so even strong proprietary models (GPT-4V, Gemini 1.5 Pro) <Problem>fail to ground high-level plans into low-level GUI actions and hallucinate that a wrong page is the goal</Problem>. Prompting/retrieval wrappers cannot exceed the frozen base model. Imitation learning on static demonstrations <Problem>cannot handle the real device/web's stochasticity and non-stationarity (pop-ups, ads, shifting layouts, reordered results) and cannot recover from the agent's own out-of-distribution mistakes</Problem>. A frozen policy's success even degrades over days as websites change (Fig.4).

## Contribution (what's new)
DigiRL is, to the authors' knowledge, the first to <Novelty>scale autonomous offline-to-online RL for in-the-wild device control</Novelty>: offline RL initializes goal-directed behavior from stale data, then online RL fine-tunes the VLM on its own fresh rollouts with autonomously-assigned rewards. The RL core is a variant of advantage-weighted regression with two design choices forced by the domain: <Novelty>a doubly-robust step-level advantage estimator</Novelty> that blends high-variance Monte-Carlo returns with a lower-variance learned value baseline to survive environment stochasticity, and <Novelty>an automatic curriculum from an instruction-level value function</Novelty> that prioritizes tasks the agent finds hard (high realized advantage) to extract maximal learning signal. Both value functions are trained with a cross-entropy (classification) loss rather than regression.

## Research questions
Can an agent learn in-the-wild device control purely from autonomous interaction (no human-labeled rollouts or hand-written verifiers), using offline data to bootstrap and online data to adapt, and beat prompting-/imitation-based agents built on much larger models?

## Methodology
Device control is cast as a finite-horizon MDP over screenshots (state = last two screenshots) and a coordinate/text/button action space, with a sparse 0/1 episode reward from a VLM evaluator. A parallel Android emulator collects rollouts; an AWR-style filtered MLE objective updates the policy on data selected by the two value functions.

## Main result
On the AitW test splits, DigiRL (1.3B, offline-to-online) reaches <Finding src="Tab.1">71.9% (General) and 67.2% (Web Shopping) success</Finding>, vs the prior best autonomous learner Filtered BC at 61.5% / 57.8% and the supervised 18B CogAgent at 25.0% / 38.5%. The abstract's headline is the +49.5% absolute jump from supervised AutoUI (17.7%) to 67.2% on Web Shopping. All gains are obtained <Finding src="§5.1">fully autonomously — no human-labeled rollouts or hand-written task verifiers</Finding>.

## Key findings
- <Finding src="Tab.1">Offline-to-online beats offline-only RL at equal sample budget</Finding>: 71.9% vs 62.8% (General test), showing fresh autonomous interaction — not just more data — drives the gain.
- <Finding src="Fig.4">A frozen good policy degrades over days while continuous online updates hold performance</Finding>: directly evidences that non-stationarity, not just initialization, requires on-the-fly learning.
- <Finding src="Fig.7">The failure mode RL reduces most is "fail to recover from mistakes"</Finding>: pre-trained/imitation agents get trapped in unseen states; training on their own rollouts teaches recovery (qualitatively, resetting to a high-value home screen, §C.2).
- <Finding src="Fig.9">Every component is necessary in ablations (Web Shopping)</Finding>: cross-entropy value loss ≈ +12% over regression, step-level advantage ≈ +12% efficiency, automatic curriculum ≈ +25% learning speed; hard filtering matches extensively-tuned exponential AWR with simpler implementation.
- <Finding src="§2">DigiRL is ~1000× more sample-efficient than prior on-policy web-nav RL</Finding>: ≈1e3 trajectories vs ≈1e6 (e.g., MiniWoB++), while operating on real GUIs.
- <Finding src="Tab.4">DigiRL produces shorter rollouts than Filtered BC on both subsets</Finding>: more directed task completion, not just higher success.
- <Finding src="Fig.9">The distributed emulator scales near-linearly to 64 emulators on 128 CPUs</Finding>: 1.74 vs 0.74 traj/min at 64 CPUs over a single-instance baseline — the engineering that makes real-time online RL feasible.

## Implications
DigiRL extends the <Related>AutoUI / CogAgent / AppAgent line of device-control agents and the autonomous-evaluation work of Pan et al. (2024)</Related> by showing weight-updating RL, not bigger frozen models, is the lever for in-the-wild GUI control. The autonomous VLM evaluator plus parallel emulator is a reusable training substrate. Worth chasing: <FollowUp>whether the offline-to-online recipe generalizes beyond AitW General/Web Shopping to GoogleApps/Install and non-Android desktop/OS environments</FollowUp>, and <FollowUp>how reward-hacking risk scales when the policy optimizes against a learned VLM judge</FollowUp>.

## Limitations
- <Caveat>Trained only on AitW General and Web Shopping subsets, not the full task space</Caveat>; GoogleApps/Install excluded for security (account/captcha) and Single excluded as single-step (§A.1) — generality to harder, longer tasks is untested.
- <Caveat>Reward and most evaluation come from the same Gemini-1.5-Pro autonomous evaluator</Caveat>; despite 2.8% mean human disagreement, the policy is optimized against a fallible judge, and General-subset evaluation is noisier than Web Shopping.
- <Caveat>Online runs are repeated only twice and offline three times (Tab.1)</Caveat>, with sizable variance on some cells (e.g., DigiRL offline Web Shopping 45.8 ± 6.6), so a few comparisons rest on thin statistics.
- <Caveat>Headline framing varies across the paper</Caveat>: abstract/Discussion cite 67.2% and a "280%" relative gain while Tab.1's best General number is 71.9% — read the per-cell table, not the prose.

## Critiques and Questions
1. The reward signal is itself a VLM; reported success could partly reflect the policy learning the evaluator's blind spots rather than true task completion — an independent held-out human eval on the final agent (beyond the correlation study) would be more convincing.
2. The text says reward is sparse/terminal, yet the doubly-robust estimator and "recover from mistakes" claim imply credit assignment to intermediate actions; how robust is `A_step > 1/H` thresholding to the choice of H (10 vs 20) and to label noise from the evaluator?
3. With only two online seeds, are the DigiRL-vs-Filtered-BC margins on Web Shopping (67.2 vs 57.8) statistically distinguishable, or within run-to-run drift caused by the very non-stationarity the paper highlights?

## Glossary
- <Term def="Advantage-weighted regression: an off-policy RL method that improves a policy by regressing it toward actions weighted by exponentiated advantage; here simplified to keep only high-advantage actions and train with a plain log-likelihood loss.">AWR</Term>
- <Term def="An estimator combining a high-variance Monte-Carlo return with a lower-variance learned value baseline so it stays accurate even if either component is imperfect.">doubly-robust advantage estimator</Term>
- <Term def="Ordering training tasks by how much learning signal they offer — here prioritizing tasks where the agent's realized return exceeds the instruction-level value function's prediction.">automatic curriculum</Term>
- <Term def="A reward/verifier that judges task success directly from observations using a VLM, without internal emulator state or per-task hand-written rules; DigiRL uses Gemini 1.5 Pro.">autonomous evaluator</Term>
- <Term def="Android in the Wild — a large dataset of Android device-control tasks; DigiRL uses its General and Web Shopping subsets.">AitW</Term>
- <Term def="Filtered behavior cloning: imitation learning on autonomously-collected rollouts keeping only successful ones; the prior best autonomous baseline.">Filtered BC</Term>
