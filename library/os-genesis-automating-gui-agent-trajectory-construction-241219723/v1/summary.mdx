## Motivation
Training VLM-based GUI agents needs high-quality trajectories — sequences of high-level instruction, low-level steps, actions, and visual/textual states. Collecting them is the bottleneck. <Problem>Human annotation is costly and labor-intensive</Problem>, requiring annotators to pre-define tasks and label entire trajectories. <Problem>Model-based synthesis depends on pre-defined high-level tasks, which limits scalability and diversity</Problem>, and <Problem>struggles to ensure quality, since errors in intermediate steps or mismatched objectives yield incomplete or incoherent trajectories</Problem>. Both produce a gap between synthetic data and real, dynamic environments.

## Contribution (what's new)
The genuine delta is <Novelty>reverse task synthesis: instead of defining a task then executing it, the agent first explores the GUI by traversing interactive elements, then retrospectively derives tasks from the observed state transitions</Novelty>. A low-level instruction is inferred from each `⟨s_pre, a, s_post⟩` triple, then generalized into a high-level goal that plausibly contains it. This design is forced by *why task-driven baselines fail*: pre-defined tasks cannot enumerate a complex environment's full interaction space and often misalign with the live UI, so grounding tasks *in* observed interactions is what makes them both executable and diverse. A second piece is the <Novelty>Trajectory Reward Model (TRM): a GPT-4o graded score in [1,5] (completion + coherence) used for probabilistic sampling rather than binary keep/discard</Novelty>, so that valuable-but-incomplete trajectories are retained instead of thrown away.

## Research questions
Can GUI agent trajectories be synthesized without human supervision or pre-defined tasks while improving downstream agent performance? Does exploration-first ("interaction-driven") construction produce more diverse and higher-quality data than task-driven synthesis, and how close does it get to human-annotated data?

## Methodology
A three-stage pipeline turns blind UI exploration into trained agents: discover functionality by interacting, invert observations into tasks, then collect and quality-weight trajectories.

## Key findings
- <Finding src="Tab.1, §4.3">OS-Genesis roughly doubles task-driven performance on AndroidWorld even with less data</Finding>: it uses 1K trajectories vs the Self-Instruct baseline's 1.5K, yet the baseline still fails to match its quality.
- <Finding src="Tab.1, §4.3">Strong out-of-distribution generalization on AndroidControl</Finding>: only 20 of the 833 apps were seen during synthesis, yet OS-Genesis consistently improves both planning (high-level) and action (low-level) over baselines and GPT-4o.
- <Finding src="Fig.4, §5.1">Highest instruction *and* trajectory diversity</Finding> (avg cosine distance) among synthetic methods. Notably, human data has high instruction diversity but low trajectory diversity — people imagine varied goals but execute with a narrow set of familiar actions — whereas OS-Genesis is high on both.
- <Finding src="Fig.5, §5.2">TRM beats both no-RM and binary-labeler filtering</Finding>, especially on high-level tasks (AndroidControl-High, AndroidWorld). A labeler helps high-level slightly but *hurts* low-level, because OS-Genesis steps are individually meaningful and shouldn't be discarded wholesale.
- <Finding src="Fig.6, §5.3">Performance scales with trajectory count then saturates</Finding> (0→1500 trajectories), attributed to VLM capacity limits and the exploration/executor ceiling of GPT-4o.
- <Finding src="Fig.7-8, §5.4">Synthesized data approaches human quality</Finding>: avg-SR retention vs human-annotated trajectories surpasses 80%, and OS-Genesis high-level instructions actually *outperform* human-written ones — pre-defined human tasks can misalign with the dynamic environment or be misread by the executor.

## Implications
Establishes interaction-first / reverse synthesis as a way to escape the pre-defined-task ceiling for GUI agent data, building on the <Related>trajectory-data lineage (Rico, MiniWob, AndroidControl, WeblinX) and online benchmarks (AndroidWorld, WebArena)</Related> and complementing grounding work like <Related>SeeClick and UGround</Related>. The TRM's graded reward over a binary labeler is a reusable idea for any synthetic-trajectory pipeline. Threads worth chasing: <FollowUp>replacing the GPT-4o explorer/annotator/TRM with open-source action models to remove proprietary dependence</FollowUp>, <FollowUp>extending reverse synthesis to desktop/OS and IDE environments beyond mobile + web</FollowUp>, and <FollowUp>whether reward-proportional sampling beats simpler reward thresholds</FollowUp>.

## Limitations
<Caveat>Proprietary dependence: exploration, annotation, and reward modeling all rely on GPT-4o</Caveat>, because current open-source VLMs cannot follow instructions and explore online environments reliably. <Caveat>Trajectory quantity is bounded by the task-executing model's capability</Caveat> — better VLMs are needed to materialize more high-level instructions into complete trajectories. <Caveat>Agents are trained/evaluated with both textual and visual representations</Caveat>; the partial (text-only or vision-only) use of full trajectory data is left to future work. Inferred: <Caveat>evaluation is limited to mobile (AndroidWorld/Control) and web (WebArena) — no desktop/OS — and main results appear to be single-run with no variance reported</Caveat>.

## Critiques and Questions
1. The TRM is GPT-4o judging GPT-4o-generated trajectories; the strong human-agreement Spearman (0.813 mobile / 0.798 web, Tab.6) is reassuring, but circularity in what "good" means could still inflate the data's apparent quality.
2. No reported variance or seeds on the headline numbers makes the InternVL2 jumps (e.g. 4.46% → 16.96% on AndroidWorld) hard to bound — how stable are they across runs?
3. "Reverse task synthesis" risks a coverage bias toward whatever the rule-based explorer happens to reach; long-horizon or multi-app tasks may be under-represented relative to short interaction chains (avg 6.4 steps).
4. WebArena absolute numbers stay low (≤10.79% overall) — does the method's advantage hold as agents approach harder, longer web workflows?
