Instead of imitating expert demonstrations, this paper uses them to induce environment-blind workflows that constrain exploration, seeding a replay buffer that trains a DOM-aware neural policy to solve sparse-reward web tasks.

An emergence-era milestone for computer-use/web agents: it introduces the durable MiniWoB++ benchmark and a DOM-structured policy, and its action-similarity exploration idea anticipates later guided agent training.

Motivation

RL on web tasks has sparse, delayed reward over a huge action space — the agent can click or type anything, a coordinated sequence is required, and one wrong action ruins the episode, so naive exploration almost never reaches reward (§1, p.1). The standard fix is to warm-start by behavioral cloning on demonstrations, but this overfits: demonstrations cover only a small slice of the diverse web state space, and prior work (Shi et al. 2017) found BC warm-start often fails to beat pure RL, while regularizing BC to fight overfitting cripples the expressivity that UI reasoning needs (§1, p.1).

Contribution (what’s new)

Use demonstrations to constrain exploration, not as an imitation target. From each demonstration the method induces a lattice of workflows — environment-blind sequences of steps, each mapping a state to a constrained set of allowable actions — and a small policy learns which workflow to follow, sampling actions uniformly from the constrained set. Because the expressive neural policy is trained only on the successful episodes this exploration discovers (never on the demonstrations directly), it gets targeted exploration without BC-style overfitting. The other delta: neighborhoods of demonstrations are defined by action similarity rather than state similarity (§7, p.9) — intuitive for the web, where two inboxes hold different emails but “type a username into a textbox” is clearly analogous.

Research questions

Can demonstrations accelerate sparse-reward web-task learning without the overfitting of behavioral cloning, and how demonstration-efficiently? And does a DOM-structured neural policy beat a pixel-based one on browser tasks?

Methodology

Two policies feed one replay buffer. The environment-blind πw explores cheaply and dumps any reward-+1 episode into a buffer; the expressive πn trains on that buffer (off-policy, like supervised learning on optimal trajectories) plus its own on-policy rollouts. State = goal + the page as a DOM tree; actions restricted to Click(e) on a leaf element and Type(e,t) where t is drawn from the goal; reward +1 on success, -1 otherwise (§2, p.3). The agent acts on the live page via a Selenium web driver (§6.1).

Method details
  1. Induce workflows (§3) — Per demonstration, at each step t enumerate every workflow step z whose constrained set contains the demonstrated action; the cross product is a workflow lattice. Shortcut steps skip noisy/unnecessary actions and collapse equivalent consecutive ones. A compact constraint language (App.A, p.13) builds steps from element selectors: Tag, Text, Like, Near (within 30px), SameRow, SameCol, And/Class, nested at most 3 deep.
  2. Workflow exploration policy πw (§4) — Select a demonstration by goal-key match (sim = 1 if structured goals share keys, else -inf), sample a step z with prob proportional to exp(ψ_`{z,t,d}`), then sample an action uniformly from z's set. ψ are per-(step, time, demo) scalars — environment-blind, so few parameters, fast learning, overfitting-resistant; trained with REINFORCE plus a baseline term for variance reduction (Eqs.1-5).
  3. Neural policy DOMNET (§5, App.C) — DOM embedder concatenates base, spatial-neighbor (within 30px), tree-neighbor (least-common-ancestor depth k=3..6, max-pooled), and goal-matching embeddings, then a series of attentions: DOM self-attention context, two-head sentinel goal attention, two-head hard DOM-element selection, then typed-string/action-type selection — producing pi(a|s) and value V(s). Goals are summed word embeddings (structured) or an LSTM (natural language).
  4. Train — A2C (synchronous advantage actor-critic) with mixed on-policy and off-policy (replay) updates; only reward-+1 episodes enter the buffer.

Main result

On MiniWoB (40 of 80 tasks, filtered to the click/type action space), DOMNET+WGE beats the pixel-based SHI17 baseline on all but two tasks and effectively solves a vast majority (Fig.3, p.6). The headline efficiency claim: WGE trained on 10 demonstrations beats BC+RL trained on 1000 demonstrations on every evaluated hard task (Fig.4, p.8) (alaska, click-checkboxes-large, email-inbox-nl, social-media), i.e. >100x demonstration efficiency over behavioral cloning (§6.3.4, p.9).

Key findings

  • WGE beats BC+RL by +42% absolute success on average across MiniWoB++ (§6.3.1, p.7): the gain concentrates on the harder tasks, while easy tasks (e.g. multi-layout 100 vs 99) are near-saturated for both.
  • BC+RL has two specific failure modes WGE avoids (§6.3.1, p.7): premature episode termination (hitting “Submit” before all boxes are checked, because that action appears in every demo) and cyclic check/uncheck loops — both symptoms of overfitting to the demonstrations.
  • πw alone is usually poor, so the neural policy does the heavy lifting (Table 1, p.6): e.g. social-media 2, email-inbox 3, email-inbox-nl 0 for πw-only, yet WGE reaches 100/99/93 — the workflow policy only needs to be constrained enough to occasionally discover reward, not to solve the task.
  • The method extends to natural-language goals (email-inbox-nl: 93%) (§6.3.2, p.7), with πn seeing only the utterance at test time; but πw still needs the structured goal because the constraint language operates on structured inputs.
  • On the realistic Alaska flight-booking task it scales to long horizons (§6.3.3, p.8): 0.97 reward with 1 demonstration on Alaska-Shi17 (vs Shi et al.’s 0.57 with ~80 demos), and 0.86 with 10 demos on a harder variant with >200 DOM elements where a random agent’s success probability is < 10^-20.
  • A real ceiling on long multi-target tasks (Table 1, p.6): social-media-all (12 steps, act on all matching tweets) is 0% for WGE — the approach does not crack the longest-horizon task.

Implications

Establishes the action-similarity-exploration framing and ships two lasting artifacts — the MiniWoB++ benchmark and the DOM-aware DOMNET architecture — that anchor a long line of web-agent work. It directly overcomes the sparse-reward failure that blocked Shi et al. 2017 (World of Bits / MiniWoB), and distinguishes itself from the demonstration-RL line (DQfD, DDPGfD, Nair et al. 2017, which pre-train or mix in demos) and from demonstration-neighborhood methods that use state similarity (Brys et al. 2015, Levine & Koltun 2013). How later MiniWoB++ agents (CC-Net / Humphreys et al. 2022, WebGUM, WebShop) and LLM-driven agents resolve the DOM-vs-pixel debate and the long-horizon ceiling. Extending the constraint language to operate directly on utterances, removing πw’s dependence on structured goals.

Limitations

  • πw is environment-blind, so it cannot adapt to layout variation; the constraint language lacks expressivity (e.g. synonyms in click-checkboxes-soft) and cannot always select the right workflow for a goal (§6.3.1).
  • Efficiency is measured in demonstrations, not environment steps: compute cost and the (likely larger) number of exploration rollouts WGE consumes are not reported, so the “100x” is a lower-bound inference from a discrete 10-vs-1000 demo grid on a subset of hard tasks, not a continuous curve.
  • No variance, seeds, or significance testing: all success rates are single-run point estimates.
  • Restricted Click/Type-from-goal action space and a 40/80 MiniWoB subset: excludes drag, scroll, free-form text, etc., so “new SOTA” is on the action-compatible subset; no safety/permission discussion for acting on live pages.

Critiques and Questions

  1. How much environment interaction does WGE’s exploration actually cost relative to BC’s? “Demonstration efficiency” could mask far higher rollout/compute cost — the load-bearing claim would be much stronger with a steps-vs-reward curve.
  2. The social-media-all 0% suggests the workflow lattice can’t seed reward at all for very long multi-target episodes. Is this a lattice-coverage problem (πw never discovers a success) or a πn credit-assignment problem? The paper does not disentangle these.
  3. With no variance reported, how stable are the near-100 results across seeds, especially for tasks where BC+RL and WGE are close (multi-layout)?
Why does the expressive neural policy πn avoid the overfitting that cripples behavioral cloning, even though both ultimately learn from the same demonstrations?
πn is never trained on the demonstrations directly. Demonstrations only shape πw's exploration; πn trains on the replay buffer of reward-+1 episodes that exploration discovers. So it learns from successful trajectories in the actual environment rather than memorizing the narrow slice of states the demos cover.

Personal notes

The most transferable idea is the inversion of how demonstrations are used: constrain where to explore (by action similarity) instead of what to output (imitation). The environment-blind πw with per-(step,time) scalar parameters is almost embarrassingly simple yet sufficient to seed the replay buffer — a reminder that the exploration helper need not be expressive, only well-constrained. Watch the gap: sample efficiency is in demonstrations, not environment steps; exploration cost is unquantified.
