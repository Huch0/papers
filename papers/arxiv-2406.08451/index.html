GUIOdyssey is the first large cross-app mobile GUI navigation dataset (8,334 episodes, 15.3 steps/episode) with step-level semantic-reasoning annotations, paired with OdysseyAgent, a Qwen-VL agent whose history resampler compresses past-screenshot tokens for efficient long-horizon navigation.

Foundational for the computer-use / mobile-agent track: it isolates the cross-app setting (context transfer across multiple apps) that single-app datasets cannot capture, and it quantifies how much historical context and reasoning annotations matter for long-horizon agents.

Motivation

Prior mobile GUI agents are trained on datasets whose tasks complete within a single app, so they perform poorly when a task spans multiple apps and requires transferring context/data between them. Existing cross-app work is evaluation-only and reports that cross-app performance lags single-app performance, and existing datasets have short episodes and lack step-level reasoning annotations, leaving a gap: no dedicated training corpus for long-horizon, cross-app mobile navigation.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is a dataset, not a model. GUIOdyssey is the first cross-app mobile navigation dataset with long episodes (15.3 steps) and per-step semantic-reasoning annotations — screen description, contextual information (summary of prior steps), and decision rationale, generated by GPT-4o and built to teach an agent to observe, review history, and reason before acting. On the model side, OdysseyAgent’s history resampler is a single-layer cross-attention module whose learnable queries attend over historical screenshot tokens to compress them (1024→256 tokens for 4 steps); this form is forced by the cross-app setting, where naively stacking many past screenshots is too slow for an interactive agent.

Research questions

  1. Can a dedicated cross-app dataset + agent close the single-app/cross-app capability gap? 2. How much do the three historical signals (actions, screenshots, contextual summaries) each contribute? 3. Do step-level semantic-reasoning annotations improve in- and out-of-domain navigation?

Methodology

High-level instruction templates are co-authored by human participants and GPT-4 (91 templates), diversified by swapping items, app pools, and GPT-4 rewrites; independent annotators demonstrate each episode on an Android emulator across 6 devices (e.g., Pixel Pro, Tablet, Fold); GPT-4o then adds low-level per-step instructions and the three-part semantic annotations; a three-criterion quality check filters bad episodes. OdysseyAgent fine-tunes Qwen-VL-Chat with the history resampler.

Method details

Episode formalism. An episode is E = {(X_t, A_t)_{t=1..T}, I_user} where X_t is the screenshot, A_t the action, and I_user the high-level instruction (§3.1).

Action set (9 actions, Tab.5). CLICK [pos1], LONG PRESS [pos1], SCROLL [pos1, pos2], TYPE text, plus HOME, BACK, RECENT (go to previous app), COMPLETE, and IMPOSSIBLE (annotator records the reason).

Six task categories (§3.2, §8.1). General Tool, Information Management, Web Shopping, Media Entertainment, Social Sharing, and Multi-Apps (3+ apps).

Semantic annotation pipeline (§8.3). GPT-4o runs iteratively per step: it first generates contextual information from prior steps’ actions/rationales, then uses that plus the current screenshot/action to produce screen description and decision rationale. CLICK/LONG PRESS steps get an extra bounding-box image; image fidelity set to ‘high’.

OdysseyAgent architecture (§4, Fig.5). Qwen-VL-Chat (ViT vision encoder + VL-adapter + LLM) plus a history resampler: a single-layer cross-attention module with learnable query embeddings as queries and historical screenshot tokens as keys/values. Compressed history tokens are concatenated with the current screen tokens, instruction, and previous actions, then fed to the LLM. Objective: L = sum_i P_theta(A_t^i | X_{t..t-delta}, I_user, A_t^{<i}) with historical window delta = 4.

Training (§9.2). AdamW, lr 2e-5, cosine schedule, beta1=0.9, beta2=0.95, weight decay 0.1, global batch 128, DeepSpeed ZeRO-2, 1 epoch, ~32 A100 hours; each action step is one training sample; semantic annotations are injected only at training time as single-turn QA pairs.

Evaluation (§5.1). Offline. Action Matching Score (AMS): correct if action type matches; CLICK/LONG PRESS correct within 14% screen distance OR if the predicted point falls inside the SAM2-segmented target region; SCROLL by direction; TYPE by ANLS > 0.5 threshold. Success Rate (SR): an episode counts only if every action is correct. Four splits: in-domain Test-Random (80/20), and out-of-domain Test-Task, Test-Device (Tablet held out), Test-App (rare apps held out).

Main result

On the in-domain Test-Random split, the semantic-annotation-trained agent reaches OdysseyAgent* = 78.24 high-level / 88.15 low-level AMS overall (Tab.2), beating the best zero-shot proprietary model (Claude3.5-Sonnet with OmniParser: 32.88 HL / 63.91 LL (Tab.2)) by a wide margin, and edging out fine-tuned baselines:

Key findings

  • Fine-tuning on GUIOdyssey is decisive (Tab.2): fine-tuned agents (~75–78 HL AMS) outperform even OmniParser-augmented Claude3.5-Sonnet (32.88 HL) by ~2x, confirming a large domain gap between single-app-trained models and cross-app tasks; off-the-shelf GUI models CogAgent/SphAgent score only ~14–16 HL AMS.
  • Contextual summaries are the single most valuable history signal (Tab.4): adding contextual information alone lifts overall AMS by 9.17 and SR by 240% over the no-history baseline, and beats using actions+screenshots together — abstracted text history generalizes better than raw frames.
  • All three history signals combined are best (Tab.4): action + screenshot + context reaches 66.84 overall AMS / 6.32 SR vs 55.60 / 1.49 for no history.
  • Decision rationale matters more than screen description or context for reasoning (Tab.6): among semantic annotations, reasoning-before-acting (DR) gives the biggest single-component gain; all three together yield +3.14 AMS and +35% SR over no annotations.
  • High-level instructions generalize worse out-of-domain than low-level ones (Tab.3): OOD drops HL AMS by ~15–16 vs only ~6–7 for LL, indicating the agent still lacks reasoning/planning to handle unseen high-level tasks; episode-level SR stays very low OOD (e.g., 0.09–0.26 on Test-Task HL).
  • The history resampler trades almost no accuracy for large efficiency gains (Tab.7): 256 vs 1024 history tokens, faster TTFT (0.71 vs 0.98) and higher TPS (20.27 vs 17.05) at 32 vs 48 GPU-hours, with AMS within ~1.5 points of multi-image concatenation.

Implications

GUIOdyssey makes cross-app mobile navigation a trainable problem rather than an evaluation-only curiosity, extending the AITW / AndroidControl / AMEX mobile-agent dataset lineage to longer, multi-app episodes and adding the reasoning-annotation idea seen in AITZ’s chain-of-action-thought. The contextual-information result is a concrete signal for memory design in long-horizon agents. Whether abstracted textual history beats raw screenshots holds for stronger base models and real online (not offline-AMS) evaluation, and how to close the persistently low episode-level Success Rate on out-of-domain high-level tasks, are the threads to chase.

Limitations

  • Evaluation is entirely offline action-matching (AMS/SR), not interactive execution in a live environment, so single correct-action errors compound and absolute SR is very low (often <12% even in-domain).
  • The dataset is built on an Android emulator with temporary accounts, so it may not capture real-device noise, latency, or authentic-account states.
  • Semantic annotations and low-level instructions are GPT-4o-generated, inheriting that model’s biases despite the quality check.
  • Out-of-domain transfer is weak, especially on Tablet (weakest device transfer due to UI-layout differences) and on unseen high-level tasks.
  • Coordinate-based actions are acknowledged as fragile relative to A11y-tree approaches.

Critiques and Questions

  1. AMS gives partial credit (e.g., 14% screen-distance tolerance, SAM2 region hit, ANLS > 0.5), which can inflate apparent competence; the brutal SR numbers are the more honest measure and suggest these agents rarely complete a full cross-app task.
  2. The 200-episode subsample used for closed-source models (budget constraint) makes the proprietary-vs-fine-tuned comparison less directly comparable to the full-test fine-tuned numbers.
  3. Semantic annotations are injected only at training time — it is unclear how much of the gain would survive if they had to be produced at inference, where no oracle context summary exists.
Among the three historical signals (actions, screenshots, contextual information), which contributes most, and why is that surprising?
Contextual information alone gives the largest gain (+9.17 AMS, +240% SR over no-history) and even beats actions+screenshots combined (Tab.4). It is surprising because an abstracted text summary of prior steps generalizes better than the raw historical screenshots themselves, suggesting summarization helps the model handle unseen GUI states.
