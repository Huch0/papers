DigiRL fine-tunes a 1.3B VLM into a state-of-the-art Android GUI agent via autonomous offline-then-online RL, lifting Web Shopping success from 17.7% to 67.2% by learning from its own real-device rollouts under an automatic VLM-based reward.

Foundational for the computer-use-agent track: it shows weight-updating RL on real, non-stationary GUIs beats prompting/imitation wrappers around far larger proprietary VLMs, and supplies a reusable autonomous-evaluation + parallel-emulator training loop.

Motivation

Off-the-shelf VLMs are under-trained on decision-centric data, so even strong proprietary models (GPT-4V, Gemini 1.5 Pro) fail to ground high-level plans into low-level GUI actions and hallucinate that a wrong page is the goal. Prompting/retrieval wrappers cannot exceed the frozen base model. Imitation learning on static demonstrations cannot handle the real device/web’s stochasticity and non-stationarity (pop-ups, ads, shifting layouts, reordered results) and cannot recover from the agent’s own out-of-distribution mistakes. A frozen policy’s success even degrades over days as websites change (Fig.4).

Contribution (what’s new)

DigiRL is, to the authors’ knowledge, the first to scale autonomous offline-to-online RL for in-the-wild device control: offline RL initializes goal-directed behavior from stale data, then online RL fine-tunes the VLM on its own fresh rollouts with autonomously-assigned rewards. The RL core is a variant of advantage-weighted regression with two design choices forced by the domain: a doubly-robust step-level advantage estimator that blends high-variance Monte-Carlo returns with a lower-variance learned value baseline to survive environment stochasticity, and an automatic curriculum from an instruction-level value function that prioritizes tasks the agent finds hard (high realized advantage) to extract maximal learning signal. Both value functions are trained with a cross-entropy (classification) loss rather than regression.

Research questions

Can an agent learn in-the-wild device control purely from autonomous interaction (no human-labeled rollouts or hand-written verifiers), using offline data to bootstrap and online data to adapt, and beat prompting-/imitation-based agents built on much larger models?

Methodology

Device control is cast as a finite-horizon MDP over screenshots (state = last two screenshots) and a coordinate/text/button action space, with a sparse 0/1 episode reward from a VLM evaluator. A parallel Android emulator collects rollouts; an AWR-style filtered MLE objective updates the policy on data selected by the two value functions.

Method details
  1. Environment & reward — Scalable, parallelized Android emulator (up to 64 emulators on 128 CPUs, near-linear speedup, Fig.9 right) with a general-purpose autonomous evaluator extending Pan et al. 2024, backed by Gemini 1.5 Pro and seeded with few-shot human-labeled rollouts; average 2.8% disagreement with humans (Fig.8). A reward of 1 is given iff the evaluator judges the task complete.
  2. Backbone — AWR with hard filtering — Advantage-weighted regression regresses the policy toward exp(A/β)-weighted actions; to avoid tuning β, DigiRL hard-filters by advantage and trains with an MLE loss L(π) = −E_filter(ν)[log π(a|s,c)] (Eq.4.2).
  3. Doubly-robust step advantage — A_step blends Monte-Carlo final reward with a learned step-value baseline (a simplified GAE with only next-step and final-step terms, since rewards are terminal); actions are kept when A_step > 1/H (Eq.4.3).
  4. Automatic curriculum — An instruction-level value function gives A_instruct = r(s_H,a_H,c) − V_instruct(c) (Eq.4.4); the top-p trajectories by A_instruct (hard, high-signal tasks) are kept before the AWR update, akin to prioritized replay / level replay.
  5. Value-function training — Both value functions use a cross-entropy objective on the Monte-Carlo trajectory reward (Eq.4.5–4.6); architecture = frozen AutoUI image encoder + RoBERTa instruction features → 2-layer MLP.
  6. Data & policy — Policy = AutoUI-Base (≈1.3B: 200M LM + 1.1B vision encoder), image encoder frozen. Tasks from AitW General (545 train / 96 test, H=10) and Web Shopping (438 train / 96 test, H=20). Offline runs use ≈1500 trajectories; offline-to-online starts from ≈500 offline + ≈1000 online trajectories, held roughly equal in total.
A_{step}(s_h,a_h,c) = \lambda^{H-h} r(s_H,a_H,c) + (1-\lambda^{H-h} r(s_H,a_H,c))\,(V_{step}(s_{h+1},c)+r(s_h,a_h,c)-V_{step}(s_h,c))

Main result

On the AitW test splits, DigiRL (1.3B, offline-to-online) reaches 71.9% (General) and 67.2% (Web Shopping) success (Tab.1), vs the prior best autonomous learner Filtered BC at 61.5% / 57.8% and the supervised 18B CogAgent at 25.0% / 38.5%. The abstract’s headline is the +49.5% absolute jump from supervised AutoUI (17.7%) to 67.2% on Web Shopping. All gains are obtained fully autonomously — no human-labeled rollouts or hand-written task verifiers (§5.1).

Key findings

  • Offline-to-online beats offline-only RL at equal sample budget (Tab.1): 71.9% vs 62.8% (General test), showing fresh autonomous interaction — not just more data — drives the gain.
  • A frozen good policy degrades over days while continuous online updates hold performance (Fig.4): directly evidences that non-stationarity, not just initialization, requires on-the-fly learning.
  • The failure mode RL reduces most is “fail to recover from mistakes” (Fig.7): pre-trained/imitation agents get trapped in unseen states; training on their own rollouts teaches recovery (qualitatively, resetting to a high-value home screen, §C.2).
  • Every component is necessary in ablations (Web Shopping) (Fig.9): cross-entropy value loss ≈ +12% over regression, step-level advantage ≈ +12% efficiency, automatic curriculum ≈ +25% learning speed; hard filtering matches extensively-tuned exponential AWR with simpler implementation.
  • DigiRL is ~1000× more sample-efficient than prior on-policy web-nav RL (§2): ≈1e3 trajectories vs ≈1e6 (e.g., MiniWoB++), while operating on real GUIs.
  • DigiRL produces shorter rollouts than Filtered BC on both subsets (Tab.4): more directed task completion, not just higher success.
  • The distributed emulator scales near-linearly to 64 emulators on 128 CPUs (Fig.9): 1.74 vs 0.74 traj/min at 64 CPUs over a single-instance baseline — the engineering that makes real-time online RL feasible.

Implications

DigiRL extends the AutoUI / CogAgent / AppAgent line of device-control agents and the autonomous-evaluation work of Pan et al. (2024) by showing weight-updating RL, not bigger frozen models, is the lever for in-the-wild GUI control. The autonomous VLM evaluator plus parallel emulator is a reusable training substrate. Worth chasing: whether the offline-to-online recipe generalizes beyond AitW General/Web Shopping to GoogleApps/Install and non-Android desktop/OS environments, and how reward-hacking risk scales when the policy optimizes against a learned VLM judge.

Limitations

  • Trained only on AitW General and Web Shopping subsets, not the full task space; GoogleApps/Install excluded for security (account/captcha) and Single excluded as single-step (§A.1) — generality to harder, longer tasks is untested.
  • Reward and most evaluation come from the same Gemini-1.5-Pro autonomous evaluator; despite 2.8% mean human disagreement, the policy is optimized against a fallible judge, and General-subset evaluation is noisier than Web Shopping.
  • Online runs are repeated only twice and offline three times (Tab.1), with sizable variance on some cells (e.g., DigiRL offline Web Shopping 45.8 ± 6.6), so a few comparisons rest on thin statistics.
  • Headline framing varies across the paper: abstract/Discussion cite 67.2% and a “280%” relative gain while Tab.1’s best General number is 71.9% — read the per-cell table, not the prose.

Critiques and Questions

  1. The reward signal is itself a VLM; reported success could partly reflect the policy learning the evaluator’s blind spots rather than true task completion — an independent held-out human eval on the final agent (beyond the correlation study) would be more convincing.
  2. The text says reward is sparse/terminal, yet the doubly-robust estimator and “recover from mistakes” claim imply credit assignment to intermediate actions; how robust is A_step > 1/H thresholding to the choice of H (10 vs 20) and to label noise from the evaluator?
  3. With only two online seeds, are the DigiRL-vs-Filtered-BC margins on Web Shopping (67.2 vs 57.8) statistically distinguishable, or within run-to-run drift caused by the very non-stationarity the paper highlights?
Why does DigiRL need an online phase rather than just more offline RL data?
Because real GUIs are non-stationary: a frozen policy's success degrades over days as websites change (Fig.4), and offline-to-online beats offline-only at equal sample budget (71.9% vs 62.8% General test, Tab.1). Learning from fresh self-collected rollouts lets the agent adapt and recover from its own out-of-distribution mistakes.
