Motivation

Large Foundation Models are shifting from chatbots to agents that act, and a natural target is to drive digital systems through their GUIs the way humans do — clicking, typing, tapping, since GUIs are ubiquitous. But the field is fragmented: benchmarks, metrics, architectures, and training recipes are scattered across papers with no shared framing, and GUIs pose hard problems like dynamic layouts, cross-platform visual diversity, and fine-grained grounding of small, numerous, scattered elements. The survey exists to impose structure on this rapidly growing but disorganized literature.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is organizational, not empirical. The survey proposes a unified taxonomy that decomposes any GUI agent into four capabilities — perception, reasoning, planning, acting — and a parallel split of the surrounding ecosystem into benchmarks, evaluation metrics, architectures, and training methods. It also formalizes the GUI-agent task as a POMDP (U, A, S, O, T) with a policy that conditions on the full action-observation history, giving the field a common formal object. The framework form follows from the failure mode of prior surveys: enumerating systems alone hides the axes (interface type, world assumption, knowledge source) along which designs actually differ.

Research questions

How should the GUI-agent space be carved up so that disparate systems become comparable — specifically, what are the recurring axes of (1) how agents perceive a screen, (2) how they reason and plan, (3) how they act on elements, (4) how they are evaluated, and (5) how they are trained — and what remains unsolved?

Methodology

A structured literature survey. Each axis of the taxonomy is populated with representative systems and contrasted along design trade-offs (privacy, robustness, reproducibility, cost), rather than re-running any experiments.

Taxonomy details

Benchmarks (§3). Two orthogonal axes. First, static datasets (fixed input-output pairs) vs interactive environments (stateful, MDP/POMDP-like, actions change state). Second, closed-world (all knowledge needed is inside the benchmark) vs open-world (task info may live outside, e.g. the live web).

  • Static closed-world: RUSS, Mind2Web, MT-Mind2Web, TURKINGBENCH, VisualWebBench, ScreenSpot, WONDERBREAD, EnvDistraction, NaviQAte.
  • Static open-world: GAIA, WebLINX.
  • Interactive closed-world: MiniWoB, CompWoB, WebShop, WebArena, VisualWebArena, WorkArena(++), ST-WebAgentBench, VideoWebArena, OS-World, Spider2-V, BrowserGym.
  • Interactive open-world: WebVLN, WebVoyager, AutoWebGLM, MMInA, WebCanvas.

Evaluation metrics (§3.3). Task-completion (goal-state reached / exact match / all subtasks done, with partial-credit variants; WebVoyager uses GPT-4V as judge with 85.3% human agreement), intermediate-step (URL / element-path / element-value matching, intent match, element accuracy, step success rate), plus efficiency, generalization, safety, and robustness metrics.

Architectures (§4).

  • Perception — four interface types: accessibility-based (semantic UI tree, layout-robust, privacy-light, but spotty for dynamic/custom widgets), HTML/DOM-based (rich structure but noisy; needs pruning, e.g. AutoWebGLM, HTML-T5), screen-visual-based (screenshots; OmniParser, TAG, Cradle — no instrumentation needed but privacy/compute cost), and hybrid (combine all three for error recovery; OS-Atlas, UGround).
  • Reasoning — WebPilot, WebOccam, OSCAR (code generation), LAST.
  • Planning — internal-knowledge (WebDreamer world-model simulation, MobA two-level, Agent S hierarchical) vs external-knowledge (Search-Agent A*, AgentQ MCTS, Toolchain, WMA world models).
  • Acting — text/metadata-based grounding (WebAgent, Mind2Web, AppAgent, MobileAgent) vs visual-only pixel grounding (UGround, OmniParser, OS-Atlas).

Training (§5). Prompt-based (no parameter updates: self-reflection in Agent Q / OSCAR, intent discovery in Auto-Intent, state-space exploration, app memory in AutoDroid) vs training-based: pre-training (PIX2STRUCT, continual pre-training of VLMs — SeeClick, UGround, CogAgent, ShowUI), fine-tuning (Falcon-UI, VGA, UI-Pro for grounding/hallucination reduction), and reinforcement learning (WebGPT, WebRL, AutoGLM, DigiRL).

Main result

This is a survey; there is no single benchmarked headline result. Per-system performance numbers are Not reported here by design — the contribution is the framework and catalog, not a leaderboard.

Key findings

  • Perception interfaces fall into four types — accessibility, HTML/DOM, screen-visual, hybrid — each with a distinct privacy/robustness/instrumentation trade-off (§4.1): accessibility and DOM are layout-robust but require developer cooperation and preprocessing, screen-visual needs no instrumentation but raises privacy and compute cost, and hybrid systems (OS-Atlas, UGround) win by falling back between modalities when one is incomplete.
  • Benchmarks separate cleanly along two axes — static dataset vs interactive environment, and closed-world vs open-world (§3): realism and reproducibility trade off directly, since live open-world sites give realism but break reproducibility.
  • Acting is moving from text/metadata grounding toward visual-only pixel grounding (§4.4): metadata (HTML/accessibility trees) is noisy and costly to parse every step, so systems like UGround and OS-Atlas map instructions straight to screen coordinates for universal cross-platform grounding.
  • Training splits into prompt-based (no weight updates) vs training-based (pre-training / fine-tuning / RL) (§5): the trend in training-based work is continual pre-training of existing VLMs then fine-tuning on curated GUI data, with RL increasingly used for error recovery and sparse-reward mitigation (WebRL, AutoGLM, DigiRL).
  • Task-completion rate dominates evaluation but is defined inconsistently and is too coarse (§3.3): definitions range from goal-state stop to exact match to all-subtasks-done, and fine-grained step metrics (URL/element/value matching) are harder to label, so cross-paper comparison is unreliable.
  • User-intent understanding is a concrete bottleneck (§6): agents reach only 51.1% accuracy on unseen websites, the survey’s clearest quantitative evidence that generalization is unsolved.

Implications

The framework gives the field a shared coordinate system: any new agent can be located by its perception interface, knowledge source for planning, action-grounding style, and training recipe. It positions the visual-grounding lineage (SeeClick → UGround → OS-Atlas) and the interactive-environment lineage (MiniWoB → WebArena → OS-World / BrowserGym) as the two backbones of current progress. Threads worth chasing: standardizing task-completion semantics so leaderboards are comparable, privacy-preserving perception (local processing, differential privacy) for agents that touch credentials and documents, and inference-latency reductions (quantization, multi-token / predictive decoding) for real-time deployment.

Limitations

  • Scope is deliberately GUI-only — CLI- and API-based agents are excluded, so it does not cover the full landscape of LFM-driven system interaction.
  • It is descriptive, not quantitative — it aggregates and contrasts but reports no head-to-head numbers, so it cannot say which design actually wins.
  • Fast-moving field, fixed snapshot: a survey of a field with this velocity ages quickly (the arXiv text spans v1 Dec 2024 through a v3 Sep 2025 revision).
  • The taxonomy’s sections are uneven — perception and benchmarks are richly detailed while reasoning is covered by only a handful of systems.

Critiques and Questions

  1. The four-pillar framework is intuitive but its boundaries blur in practice — reasoning vs planning, and perception vs acting (visual grounding sits in both), overlap, so the taxonomy may classify systems ambiguously.
  2. The single quantitative claim (51.1% intent accuracy on unseen sites) is borrowed from one cited paper, not a normalized cross-benchmark measurement; the survey offers no apples-to-apples comparison of any capability.
  3. With benchmarks defined and evaluated so inconsistently (the survey itself flags this), how much does the elegant taxonomy actually help a practitioner pick a system today?
What are the two orthogonal axes the survey uses to organize GUI-agent benchmarks?
Static dataset vs interactive environment (does taking an action change state?), and closed-world vs open-world (is all task knowledge contained inside the benchmark, or can it live outside, e.g. on the live web?).
