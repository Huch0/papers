Motivation

Training VLM-based GUI agents needs high-quality trajectories — sequences of high-level instruction, low-level steps, actions, and visual/textual states. Collecting them is the bottleneck. Human annotation is costly and labor-intensive, requiring annotators to pre-define tasks and label entire trajectories. Model-based synthesis depends on pre-defined high-level tasks, which limits scalability and diversity, and struggles to ensure quality, since errors in intermediate steps or mismatched objectives yield incomplete or incoherent trajectories. Both produce a gap between synthetic data and real, dynamic environments.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is reverse task synthesis: instead of defining a task then executing it, the agent first explores the GUI by traversing interactive elements, then retrospectively derives tasks from the observed state transitions. A low-level instruction is inferred from each ⟨s_pre, a, s_post⟩ triple, then generalized into a high-level goal that plausibly contains it. This design is forced by why task-driven baselines fail: pre-defined tasks cannot enumerate a complex environment’s full interaction space and often misalign with the live UI, so grounding tasks in observed interactions is what makes them both executable and diverse. A second piece is the Trajectory Reward Model (TRM): a GPT-4o graded score in [1,5] (completion + coherence) used for probabilistic sampling rather than binary keep/discard, so that valuable-but-incomplete trajectories are retained instead of thrown away.

Research questions

Can GUI agent trajectories be synthesized without human supervision or pre-defined tasks while improving downstream agent performance? Does exploration-first (“interaction-driven”) construction produce more diverse and higher-quality data than task-driven synthesis, and how close does it get to human-annotated data?

Methodology

A three-stage pipeline turns blind UI exploration into trained agents: discover functionality by interacting, invert observations into tasks, then collect and quality-weight trajectories.

Method details
  1. Interaction-driven functional discovery — Human-free, mostly rule-based traversal of interactive elements in live environments (Android emulator + Chrome browser) using actions a ∈ {CLICK, TYPE, SCROLL}. GPT-4o is invoked only to fill input fields with contextually appropriate content. Produces massive ⟨s_pre, a, s_post⟩ triples (pre/post-action screenshots + action).
  2. Reverse task synthesis — An annotation model (GPT-4o) maps each triple → an atomic low-level instruction τ_low (flow: ⟨s_pre,a,s_post⟩ → τ_low), then generalizes τ_low → a high-level goal τ_high that plausibly encompasses it (fhigh: τ_low → τ_high). Yields a diverse instruction set T={τ_1,…,τ_N}, fully without human intervention.
  3. Trajectory collection + TRM — GPT-4o executes the synthesized high-level instructions in the environment to produce complete trajectories G. The TRM (GPT-4o) scores each trajectory R∈[1,5] on completion and coherence, using the low-level instructions and the last three states; trajectories are then sampled for training with probability proportional to reward (Algorithm 1).
  4. Trajectory training (SFT) — Full fine-tuning of VLMs (InternVL2-4B/8B, Qwen2-VL-7B) on 8×A100 80GB. Two objectives: Planning Training (predict low-level instruction + action given state/high-level/history) and Action Training (predict action given low-level instruction). Agents emit ReAct-style outputs. Task-Driven and OS-Genesis use 1K trajectories; Self-Instruct uses 1.5K; avg trajectory length 6.4 steps.

The headline action space is {CLICK, TYPE, SCROLL} (plus OpenApp for mobile, seen in Fig.3). All settings uniformly accept a11ytree + screenshots as input.

Main result

On the dynamic online benchmark AndroidWorld, OS-Genesis nearly doubles the best task-driven baseline: 17.41% success rate with Qwen2-VL-7B vs 9.82% for Task-Driven w. Self-Instruct, and 23.70% for the GPT-4o M3A reference (Tab.1). Gains are consistent across all three backbones:

Key findings

  • OS-Genesis roughly doubles task-driven performance on AndroidWorld even with less data (Tab.1, §4.3): it uses 1K trajectories vs the Self-Instruct baseline’s 1.5K, yet the baseline still fails to match its quality.
  • Strong out-of-distribution generalization on AndroidControl (Tab.1, §4.3): only 20 of the 833 apps were seen during synthesis, yet OS-Genesis consistently improves both planning (high-level) and action (low-level) over baselines and GPT-4o.
  • Highest instruction and trajectory diversity (Fig.4, §5.1) (avg cosine distance) among synthetic methods. Notably, human data has high instruction diversity but low trajectory diversity — people imagine varied goals but execute with a narrow set of familiar actions — whereas OS-Genesis is high on both.
  • TRM beats both no-RM and binary-labeler filtering (Fig.5, §5.2), especially on high-level tasks (AndroidControl-High, AndroidWorld). A labeler helps high-level slightly but hurts low-level, because OS-Genesis steps are individually meaningful and shouldn’t be discarded wholesale.
  • Performance scales with trajectory count then saturates (Fig.6, §5.3) (0→1500 trajectories), attributed to VLM capacity limits and the exploration/executor ceiling of GPT-4o.
  • Synthesized data approaches human quality (Fig.7-8, §5.4): avg-SR retention vs human-annotated trajectories surpasses 80%, and OS-Genesis high-level instructions actually outperform human-written ones — pre-defined human tasks can misalign with the dynamic environment or be misread by the executor.

Implications

Establishes interaction-first / reverse synthesis as a way to escape the pre-defined-task ceiling for GUI agent data, building on the trajectory-data lineage (Rico, MiniWob, AndroidControl, WeblinX) and online benchmarks (AndroidWorld, WebArena) and complementing grounding work like SeeClick and UGround. The TRM’s graded reward over a binary labeler is a reusable idea for any synthetic-trajectory pipeline. Threads worth chasing: replacing the GPT-4o explorer/annotator/TRM with open-source action models to remove proprietary dependence, extending reverse synthesis to desktop/OS and IDE environments beyond mobile + web, and whether reward-proportional sampling beats simpler reward thresholds.

Limitations

Proprietary dependence: exploration, annotation, and reward modeling all rely on GPT-4o, because current open-source VLMs cannot follow instructions and explore online environments reliably. Trajectory quantity is bounded by the task-executing model’s capability — better VLMs are needed to materialize more high-level instructions into complete trajectories. Agents are trained/evaluated with both textual and visual representations; the partial (text-only or vision-only) use of full trajectory data is left to future work. Inferred: evaluation is limited to mobile (AndroidWorld/Control) and web (WebArena) — no desktop/OS — and main results appear to be single-run with no variance reported.

Critiques and Questions

  1. The TRM is GPT-4o judging GPT-4o-generated trajectories; the strong human-agreement Spearman (0.813 mobile / 0.798 web, Tab.6) is reassuring, but circularity in what “good” means could still inflate the data’s apparent quality.
  2. No reported variance or seeds on the headline numbers makes the InternVL2 jumps (e.g. 4.46% → 16.96% on AndroidWorld) hard to bound — how stable are they across runs?
  3. “Reverse task synthesis” risks a coverage bias toward whatever the rule-based explorer happens to reach; long-horizon or multi-app tasks may be under-represented relative to short interaction chains (avg 6.4 steps).
  4. WebArena absolute numbers stay low (≤10.79% overall) — does the method’s advantage hold as agents approach harder, longer web workflows?
Why is it called 'reverse' task synthesis, and what is the core unit it operates on?
Conventional pipelines define a task then execute it; OS-Genesis reverses this — the agent first explores the GUI and records ⟨s_pre, a, s_post⟩ interaction triples, then retrospectively derives a low-level instruction from each triple and generalizes it into a high-level task. The triple (pre-action state, action, post-action state) is the core unit.
