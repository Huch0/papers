Motivation

Existing MLLM-based GUI agents have two recurring weaknesses. First, they do only basic single-step reasoning and fail to leverage information from prior steps, so they reflect poorly on past actions and repeat the same errors. Second, they lean on textual GUI representations such as accessibility trees or Set-of-Marks, but GUIs are inherently visual — text representations lose or duplicate information, add compute overhead, and vary in availability and consistency across platforms, hindering deployment.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is making advanced reasoning native rather than prompted. The agent is trained so that two specific reasoning skills are emitted at every step from the model itself: hierarchical reasoning (a strategic planning layer plus a tactical action-selection/grounding layer) and expectation-reflection reasoning (predict the outcome of each action, then at the next step compare the realized state against that prediction). The design must take this form because a single-step prompted agent has no persistent self-evaluation signal; by synthesizing the reflection/expectation chain into SFT data, the agent learns to self-correct without any extra prompting at inference. A deliberate choice: expectations are generated without access to the next state s_{t+1}, so the model learns to handle expectation mismatches at deployment instead of producing unrealistically perfect predictions.

Research questions

Can a small MLLM be trained to (1) acquire solid GUI fundamentals (understanding + grounding) and (2) natively reason and reflect across steps, such that it competes with larger agents while operating on raw screenshots alone?

Methodology

A two-stage supervised fine-tuning pipeline on top of Qwen2-VL-2B. Stage 1 builds fundamentals from a broad data mix; Stage 2 continues training on MLLM-synthesized trajectory data that embeds the reasoning skills.

Method details

Stage 1 — fundamental abilities (~1M samples, Tab.1). Five data categories: GUI understanding (Screen2Words, Screen Annotation), grounding (GUIEnv 150k, RICO Semantic Annotation 150k, SeeClick-Web 100k, RICO SCA 100k, Widget Caption 70k, UIBert, OmniAct-Single Click), QA (GUIChat 40k, ScreenQA 17k, Complex QA 11k), general knowledge (LLaVA-OneVision 250k, PixMo 68.8k), and tool usage (Glaive-function-calling 5k). Coordinates are normalized to a relative [0, 1000] scale with origin at top-left; points are {"x":x,"y":y} and boxes are {"x1":...,"y2":...}. A Reference-Augmented Annotation format ties GUI elements to text bidirectionally; Qwen2-VL-72B is used for instruction enhancement and response refinement.

Stage 2 — native reasoning (45K synthesized samples, Tab.2). Built from trajectory datasets GUIAct (10k), AMEX (3k), Android in the Zoo (2k), plus 30k Stage-1-aligned compositions. At each step the agent runs a cycle: Reflection → Hierarchical Reasoning (Strategic: summary + planning; Tactical: action selection + grounding) → Action → Expectation. Inputs at step t are goal g, current observation o_t, and a history window H_t = {(o_i, r_i, a_i)} of size n. Interaction uses a function-call protocol (<tool_call> with name + arguments; <tool_response> with status/current_ui/current_task).

  1. Screenshot description — Generate a text description d_t per observation so non-interleaved MLLMs can reason over steps.
  2. Reflection — From prior expectation e_{t-1} and current observation o_t, judge whether the previous action met its expectation.
  3. Strategic layer — Summary from n-step history + o_t, then planning generated with access to the actual action a_t for trajectory alignment.
  4. Tactical layer — From reflection + strategic output, select/ground the concrete action; a_t is incorporated to keep tactical reasoning consistent.
  5. Expectation — Generate e_t from o_t, r_t, a_t — deliberately WITHOUT s_{t+1} to preserve robustness to mismatches.

Modular action space (Tab.3). Standardized, composable operations: single-point (tap, click, hover, select), two-point (swipe, select_text), directional (scroll), text input (input, point_input), parameterless (remember, enter, home, back), and state settings (set_task_status). An auxiliary next-state-prediction task (predict d_{t+1} from o_t, a_t) is explored to model transition dynamics while keeping expectation generation independent of future states.

Training compute. Full-parameter SFT of Qwen2-VL-2B across 8x A800 80GB GPUs using ZeRO0.

Main result

On ScreenSpot, InfiGUIAgent-2B reaches 76.3% average accuracy, surpassing ShowUI-2B (75.1%) and the larger UGround-7B (73.3%) (Tab.4, p.7), all evaluated on raw screenshots. On AndroidWorld, it scores 0.09 overall, above ShowUI-2B (0.07), Qwen2-VL-72B (0.05), and LLaVa-OV-7B (0.00) (Tab.5, p.7) — notably without using the Set-of-Marks annotations the environment provides.

Key findings

  • A 2B model beats 7B+ grounding specialists on ScreenSpot (Tab.4, p.7): InfiGUIAgent-2B (76.3%) edges out UGround-7B (73.3%) and ShowUI-2B (75.1%) despite far fewer parameters, supporting the claim that the training recipe — not scale — drives grounding quality.
  • Native reasoning helps navigation more than raw scale (Tab.5, p.7): on AndroidWorld it (0.09) outperforms Qwen2-VL-72B (0.05), a 36x larger base, indicating step-wise reflection/planning matters for multi-step task success.
  • Vision-only operation is viable (§4.1.2, p.6): the agent responds to raw images rather than the Set-of-Marks labels AndroidWorld supplies, on the argument that over-reliance on SoM weakens pixel-level grounding — yet it still leads similar-size models.
  • Gains concentrate on Easy tasks (Tab.5, p.7): the AndroidWorld 0.09 comes from Easy 0.25 with Middle 0.00 and Hard 0.00, so harder multi-step tasks remain effectively unsolved.
  • Decoupling expectations from the next state is intentional (§3.2.5, p.6): generating e_t without s_{t+1} trades peak expectation accuracy for robustness to mismatches at deployment, with a separate auxiliary task added to model transitions.
  • Heterogeneous data is unified before training (§3.1.1, p.5): coordinates normalized to [0,1000] and a Reference-Augmented Annotation format let many sources combine into ~1M Stage-1 samples with consistent spatial grounding.

Implications

Pushes the vision-only GUI-agent lineage (SeeClick, CogAgent, Ferret-UI, ShowUI, UGround) toward small models whose reasoning is internalized in weights rather than prompted, lowering deployment cost. It also extends the reflection/planning agent line (Reflexion, ReAct) by distilling those behaviors into SFT data instead of relying on inference-time scaffolds. Worth chasing: whether the expectation-reflection chain actually reduces repeated-error loops in practice (no ablation isolates it), how performance scales if the same recipe is applied to a 7B+ base, and whether the synthetic-trajectory reasoning generalizes beyond the mobile-heavy training distribution to desktop/web navigation.

Limitations

  • AndroidWorld success is low in absolute terms (0.09) and zero on Middle/Hard tasks — the agent handles only the easiest multi-step tasks.
  • No ablation isolates the contribution of hierarchical vs. expectation-reflection reasoning, or of Stage 2 over Stage 1; the “native reasoning helps” claim rests on cross-model comparison, not a controlled study.
  • Evaluation is narrow: two benchmarks, single reported runs with no variance/error bars, and a mobile-skewed training mix.
  • Stage 2 reasoning data is synthesized by Qwen2-VL-72B from existing trajectories, so quality is bounded by that teacher and by trajectory coverage.

Critiques and Questions

  1. The headline framing (“native reasoning skills enhance GUI interaction”) is under-evidenced without an ablation that turns the reasoning chain off on the same model — the comparisons are against different models with different data, so the gain could come from the Stage-1 data mix or the [0,1000] coordinate normalization rather than from reflection per se.
  2. Numbers are single-run with no variance; on AndroidWorld the small absolute values (0.07 vs 0.09) make ranking fragile.
  3. The ScreenSpot and AndroidWorld tables cover disjoint model sets, so the “competitive with larger models” story is assembled from two partial comparisons rather than one consistent leaderboard.
What does the expectation-reflection mechanism do, and why are expectations generated without access to the next state?
At each step the agent predicts the outcome of its action (the expectation); at the next step it reflects by comparing the realized observation against that prediction to detect and correct errors. Expectations are generated without s_{t+1} (§3.2.5) so the model learns to cope with mismatches at deployment, rather than learning unrealistically perfect predictions it could not reproduce at inference.
