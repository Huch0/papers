A mobile-specific survey of LLM-powered phone GUI agents that organizes the field into a three-axis taxonomy — Frameworks, Models, and Resources — and frames LLMs as the answer to four long-standing failures of script-based phone automation.

The reference map for the computer-use-agents track on the phone platform: it names the canonical systems, datasets, and benchmarks, and isolates the open problems (on-device deployment, long-horizon planning, security) worth following.

Motivation

Script-based phone automation (testing tools, shortcuts, RPA) hits four structural walls. Limited generality — scripts break when an app updates its UI or the user switches apps. High maintenance cost — frequent UI changes force constant, expert rewrites. Poor intent comprehension — rule-based systems handle “open the browser” but not “go to Amazon and buy X”. Weak screen GUI perception — fixed control-targeting fails on dynamic, API-less, non-standard interfaces. No prior survey had analyzed LLM-driven automation through a specifically mobile lens.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is a phone-centric organizing framework rather than new results: a unified three-axis taxonomy (Frameworks / Models / Resources) read through mobile-specific I/O — view hierarchies, touch action spaces, on-device constraints — plus a problem-to-solution narrative mapping each LLM capability to one of the four pre-LLM failures. The design must take this form because, per the comparison in Tab.1, prior surveys are platform-agnostic or only loosely mobile; the mobile setting (touchscreen interaction, constrained resources, diverse app ecosystem) imposes distinct trade-offs that a general GUI-agent survey cannot surface.

Research questions

What capabilities let LLMs overcome the four failures of traditional phone automation; how can the design space (framework / model / data-and-eval) be organized for the phone platform; and which trade-offs and gaps remain.

Methodology

A structured literature review. The field is decomposed into the three-axis taxonomy (Fig.2), a historical trajectory from random/model/learning-based testing to LLM agents (Fig.3), and a component view of any agent as Perception → Brain → Action (Tab.3). Per-system metrics are Not reported (N/A) — the survey curates and contrasts published systems rather than re-running them.

Taxonomy detail
  • Frameworks (§4): Single-Agent (DroidBot-GPT, AutoDroid, AppAgent, CogAgent, Mobile-Agent), Multi-Agent split into Role-Coordinated (Mobile-Agent-v2/E, Agent S2) and Scenario-Based (MobileExperts, SteP), and Plan-Then-Act (SeeAct, UGround, LiMAC). Single-agent operation is modeled as a POMDP.
  • Models (§5): Prompt Engineering (text-based vs. multimodal prompts) vs. Training-Based — task-specific architectures (Auto-GUI, CogAgent, ScreenAI, UI-TARS, GUI-Owl), Supervised Fine-Tuning (SeeClick, GUICourse, GUI Odyssey), and Reinforcement Learning (AutoGLM, UI-R1, GUI-G1, AgentCPM-GUI). RL targets are split across phone / web / PC agents.
  • Resources (§6): Datasets (Rico, PixelHelp, AITW, AITZ, AndroidControl, AMEX) and Benchmarks (AndroidWorld, LlamaTouch, B-MoCA, MobileAgentBench, A3, SPA-Bench, Mobile-Bench-v2), with a metric taxonomy spanning task-completion, action-quality, efficiency, reasoning, and reliability metrics.

Key findings

The survey’s organizing insights:

  • LLMs map onto the four failures by capability (§3.3): contextual semantic understanding fixes intent comprehension, multimodal perception fixes weak GUI perception, and reasoning/decision-making handles dynamic cross-app workflows; data scaling laws show consistent gains on step-level tasks.
  • The right framework is a component choice, not a fixed architecture (§4.6, Tab.3): viewing every agent as Perception / Brain / Action reveals the real trade-offs better than treating Single-Agent vs. Multi-Agent vs. Plan-Then-Act as rivals.
  • Perception faces a richness-vs-speed trade-off (§4.6): fusing screenshots, UI trees, SoM, and OCR gives rich context but high per-step latency, and current agents still cannot perceive dynamic content like videos.
  • The “Brain” faces a speed/memory/cost trilemma (§4.6): per-step inference takes seconds to tens of seconds (too slow for interactive use), agents have short-term but not persistent long-term memory, and powerful multi-agent setups are expensive.
  • Modeling is a generalization-vs-specialization gap (§5.3, §6.3): prompting adapts to new apps but is capped by the base model; training-based methods reach SOTA and enable on-device use but generalize poorly to unseen apps. RL consistently outperforms SFT-only, pointing to hybrid SFT-then-RL pipelines.
  • A capability gap separates offline skills from online tasks (§6.3, §7): agents do well on single-step offline tasks (e.g. visual grounding) but success rates stay low on complex multi-step online benchmarks like AndroidWorld — the field is bottlenecked by dataset/benchmark realism.
  • A distinct security threat surface is emerging (§7, Tab.15): environmental-injection attacks (AEIA) reach up to 93% success by corrupting the agent’s contextual reasoning, and backdoor attacks (VisualTrap) poison the vision foundation model so triggers survive clean fine-tuning.

Implications

This is the consolidated map for the computer-use / phone GUI agent lineage — DroidBot-GPT and AppAgent through CogAgent, UI-TARS, and the Mobile-Agent family — and it explicitly positions itself as the first mobile-focused entry alongside general GUI-agent surveys (Tab.1). Threads worth chasing: hybrid SFT-then-RL training pipelines; lightweight on-device agents (Octopus v2, TinyClick) that stay robust under real-world UI drift; compositional cross-app datasets and online benchmarks to close the offline-to-online capability gap; and end-to-end security plus Federated Learning for privacy on personal devices.

Limitations

As a survey it reports no primary metrics — per-system numbers are second-hand and the quantitative comparison is confined to the benchmark discussion in §6.3. The taxonomy and conclusions are a snapshot of a fast-moving field; the authors mitigate this with a continuously updated GitHub list, but the paper text ages quickly. The mobile-only scope is deliberate, so desktop/web agents appear only as context, and the “why LLMs help” narrative is largely qualitative rather than experimentally isolated.

Critiques and Questions

  1. The four-failures-to-four-capabilities mapping is clean but mostly asserted from cited examples; it is hard to tell how much each LLM capability independently contributes versus simply scaling.
  2. The headline capability gap (strong offline, weak online) is the most useful finding — but the survey attributes it largely to datasets/benchmarks; how much is the architecture (perception latency, missing long-term memory) versus the data is left open.
What are the three axes of the survey's taxonomy, and why is a phone-specific survey argued to be necessary?
Frameworks (single/multi-agent, plan-then-act), Models (prompt engineering vs. training-based SFT/RL), and Resources (datasets and benchmarks). Phone-specific because the mobile setting — touchscreen action space, view hierarchies, and on-device resource constraints — imposes trade-offs that platform-agnostic GUI-agent surveys (Tab.1) do not capture.
