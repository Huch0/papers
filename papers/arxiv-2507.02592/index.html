A complete open-source post-training recipe — graph-synthesized high-uncertainty QA (SailorFog-QA), reconstructed short-CoT cold-start traces, and a faster agentic RL algorithm (DUPO) — that lifts open browsing agents to near-proprietary performance on the hardest information-seeking benchmarks.

Directly on my computer-use / web-agent track: it is the first open recipe to make a browsing ReAct agent competitive with DeepResearch-class systems on BrowseComp, and its data-synthesis idea (sample obfuscated subgraphs to manufacture irreducible uncertainty) is reusable beyond web search.

Motivation

Proprietary agents like DeepResearch reach 51.5% on BrowseComp-en (Tab.1) while open-source web agents sit near zero (WebDancer-QwQ 3.8, WebThinker-RL 2.8 (Tab.1)). The paper argues this gap is not scale but missing reasoning patterns: open models are trained on Level 1/2 tasks (single-search or fixed-path multi-hop) and never face Level 3 problems with high, hard-to-reduce uncertainty and no predefined solution path. A second blocker is supervision: strong open LRMs (QwQ, DeepSeek-R1) can solve some hard tasks, but their verbose, stylized chains-of-thought are unfit for fine-tuning — they impose stylistic priors and overflow the context window on long-horizon trajectories.

Contribution (what’s new)

The genuine delta is a recipe to manufacture irreducible uncertainty and learn from it cheaply. SailorFog-QA samples diverse subgraphs from random-walk knowledge graphs seeded from rare Wikidata entities, then deliberately obfuscates facts (precise date → “early 2010s”, name → “founded by someone with the initial ‘F’”) so the agent must compare and synthesize rather than look up. Because direct imitation of LRM traces fails, reasoning is reconstructed: keep only the expert’s successful action–observation trace, discard its native thoughts, and regenerate concise “short-CoT” justifications per step — capturing the what/how without inheriting verbosity or stylistic priors. Training is two-stage: a deliberately modest RFT cold start (the paper argues SFT is indispensable here because RL rewards are near-zero-sparse at the start), then DUPO, an agentic-RL algorithm that pre-filters all-correct cases and, instead of padding the batch with fresh sequential rollouts (DAPO), duplicates within-batch samples with non-zero reward variance to cut the rollout bottleneck.

Research questions

  • Why do open-source web agents fail on BrowseComp while proprietary ones succeed, and is the missing ingredient a trainable reasoning pattern rather than model scale?
  • Can high-uncertainty Level-3 training data be synthesized scalably, and can an open agent trained on it close the gap to proprietary systems?
  • Is an SFT cold start necessary for agentic RL on long-horizon web tasks, or can RL be applied directly?

Methodology

Agent is ReAct (Thought → Action → Observation) with two tools — search (Google, top-10 titles/snippets/URLs per query) and visit (Jina fetch + Qwen-2.5-72B goal-conditioned summarization) — capped at 30 tool calls. Backbones are Qwen-2.5 at 3B/7B/32B/72B. Data is built graph-first, then RFT-filtered, then RL’d with DUPO.

Method details
  1. Graph construction (App. A.2) — Seed a fuzzy/rare entity from Wikidata SPARQL; gather features via search+visit; extract related entities/relations as nodes/edges; iteratively and probabilistically expand (either branch a new entity or revisit a prior node) until an edge budget is hit — discouraging linear chains and producing densely interconnected, non-linear topologies.
  2. SailorFog-QA generation (§3.1) — Sample subgraphs of diverse topology; write a QA over the subgraph; inject information obfuscation (vague dates, partial names, qualitative descriptors) to raise initial uncertainty. Claimed advantages: grounded in the real internet, diverse reasoning patterns, non-linear scalability in graph size.
  3. Trajectory reconstruction (§3.2) — Prompt an expert LRM (QwQ-32B) for a full solution trajectory; keep only the action–observation trace; for each step t regenerate a concise thought τ̂t ~ π*(τ | H_{t-1}, a_t, o_t) in a short-CoT style.
  4. RFT cold start (§4.1) — Three-stage filter: keep only correct-answer trajectories (rejection sampling), drop trajectories over 32k tokens, and retain only those with >5 tool calls. Mask observation tokens from the loss. Just over 2k examples.
  5. DUPO RL (§4.2) — Pre-filter cases where all 8 rollouts are correct; during training duplicate within-batch samples with non-zero reward std instead of padding with fresh rollouts; GRPO-style group-relative advantage, token-level loss, DAPO higher-clip, observation masking.

Reward is rule-based to avoid hacking:

R_i = 0.1 \cdot R^{format}_i + 0.9 \cdot R^{answer}_i

where the format term checks ReAct tag structure (<think>, <tool_call>, <answer>) and the answer term is an LLM-as-judge correctness score. RL hyperparameters: group rollout 8, temperature 1.0, batch 128 / mini-batch 32, lr 1e-6; SFT uses batch 32, lr 5e-6 (App. A.4).

Main result

On the hardest benchmarks WebSailor-72B sets a new open-source state-of-the-art and reaches parity with a top proprietary agent in Chinese: 12.0 on BrowseComp-en and 30.1 on BrowseComp-zh, vs DeepResearch 51.5 / 42.9 and Doubao —/26.0 (Tab.1, p.9). The small models punch above their weight — WebSailor-7B (6.7 on BrowseComp-en) beats 32B-backbone WebDancer-32B (2.5) and WebThinker-RL (2.8) (Tab.1, p.9), evidence the gains are from the training paradigm, not scale.

Key findings

  • Direct inference is near-useless for complex info-seeking (Tab.1, p.8): even GPT-4.1 scores 1.5 on BrowseComp-en, confirming the task needs live web interaction, not parametric knowledge.
  • WebSailor stays best even on easy Level-1 tasks (Fig.4): on a 200-sample SimpleQA subset, WebSailor-72B hits 93.5% pass@1 (vs 87.5 for WebDancer-32B), so training on hard data is “downward compatible” rather than overfit to difficulty.
  • The RFT cold start is essential, not optional (Fig.6): direct RL shows a larger raw Pass@1 increase but converges lower; the cold-started model keeps a high, stable tool-call count while direct-RL tool use stays low — i.e. without cold start the model never masters long-horizon reasoning, and the gap is widest on BrowseComp-en.
  • RL helps hardest tasks most and mostly raises Pass@1 (Fig.5): largest gains on BrowseComp-en/zh (e.g. +8.3 Pass@1 on 72B BrowseComp-zh); Pass@1 rises more than Pass@3, indicating RL improves sample efficiency / stability rather than just ceiling.
  • SailorFog-QA’s difficulty profile matches BrowseComp, unlike prior training sets (Fig.3): WebDancer’s data is >50% two-tool-call and almost never exceeds ten, whereas SailorFog-QA has a long tail past twenty tool calls mirroring BrowseComp-en.
  • The synthetic data is genuinely hard (Tab.2): before filtering, o4-mini/DeepSeek-R1 reach only 47.3/38.9 pass@1 on SailorFog-QA vs 90.2/84.4 on WebDancer-QA (and 26.3/9.5 on BrowseComp-en) — far harder than the prior dataset, partly because obfuscation can admit non-unique answers.

Implications

This is a concrete blueprint for closing the open-vs-proprietary gap on agentic info-seeking without distillation-at-scale, and the “manufacture uncertainty by sampling + obfuscating knowledge-graph subgraphs” idea is a reusable data engine. It extends the ReAct line (Yao et al., 2023) and the open web-agent lineage — WebDancer, WebThinker, R1-Searcher, Search-o1, and the RL side builds on GRPO (DeepSeekMath) and DAPO. Threads worth chasing: asynchronous RL to lift the 50-step training cap the authors flag as a bottleneck, raising the 32k-token trajectory filter to learn even longer-horizon tasks, and whether the obfuscated-subgraph data engine transfers to non-search agentic domains (e.g. computer/GUI use).

Limitations

  • The 32k-token trajectory filter may cap achievable difficulty; failure analysis shows many errors come from exceeding the context limit, and performance degrades as inference length grows (§5.4).
  • “Over-thinking”: the agent applies multi-step tool calls even to simple questions — though the authors argue this is often cross-verification rather than aimless exploration (§5.4).
  • RL is limited to 50 steps because the synchronous framework is slow even with DUPO (§5.4).
  • Weaker on GAIA’s math/computation-heavy tasks since WebSailor is not optimized for them; the headline GAIA number mixes these with info-retrieval subsets (§5.2).
  • Obfuscated questions can lack a unique answer, so even “correct” supervision is noisy — the authors only guarantee the answer satisfies the stated constraints, not uniqueness (§5.3, Tab.2).

Critiques and Questions

  1. Absolute numbers are still low (12.0 on BrowseComp-en) and well behind DeepResearch’s 51.5 — “closing the gap” holds mainly for BrowseComp-zh parity with Doubao, not the English SOTA. The framing of “superhuman” rests on the claim that the synthetic tasks are intractable for humans in two hours, which is a small manual study, not a controlled measurement.
  2. Many comparison cells are missing (”−” due to cost/API access), so the proprietary comparison is partial and partly self-reported / website-evaluated (Tab.1 footnote ‡).
  3. All metrics are pass@1 from a single-temperature decode (T=0.6) with an LLM judge for correctness; no variance/CIs are given on the main table, and the judge itself is unaudited — both matter on a benchmark with ~zero baselines where small absolute swings dominate.
  4. The reconstructed-reasoning supervision could leak the answer (thoughts are generated given the correct action and observation), risking traces that look decisive only in hindsight.
Why does WebSailor reconstruct reasoning traces instead of fine-tuning directly on expert LRM thoughts?
Expert LRMs (e.g. QwQ-32B) produce verbose, stylized chains-of-thought that (a) impose stylistic priors that stifle the trainee's own exploratory strategies and (b) overflow the context window on long-horizon, dozens-of-tool-call trajectories. So they keep only the successful action–observation trace and regenerate concise short-CoT justifications per step, capturing solution logic without the drawbacks.
